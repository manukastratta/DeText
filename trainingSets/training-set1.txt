culture</h1> </div> </div> </div> </div> </div> <div class="pb-layout-item pb-chain pb-c-default-chain col-xs-12 col-md-9" id="c5hhvW1uC49rfr"> <div class="wrapper pb-feature pb-layout-item pb-f-article-social-buttons" id="f0TdLO3uC49rfr"> <div class="social-buttons"> <script src="//s7.addthis.com/js/300/addthis_widget.js#pubid=ra-53cad487251df74f" async></script> <div class="wrapper pb-feature pb-layout-item pb-f-article-article-body" id="fST2k82uC49rfr"> <div class="row"> <div class="col-xs-12"> <div class="ab-article-body"> <div class="ab-byline"> By <a class="contr-link" href="http://columbiaspectator.com/contributors/Laura-Salgado/">Laura Salgado</a> <span>| April 9, 2019, 1:29 PM</span> </div> <div class="ab-article-content"> <article> <p><i>Content warning: This column contains references to sexual assault which may be upsetting to some readers.</i></p> <p>When Brett Kavanaugh was being confirmed to the Supreme Court last September, I watched all the hearings with grim fascination. I listened as Christine Blasey Ford detailed her alleged attack at the hands of Kavanaugh, horrified but not all that surprised that her alleged attacker was dangerously close to a lifetime appointment to the most powerful court in the country—harking back to Anita Hill’s testimony against Clarence Thomas 27 years before. I thought about the role elite institutions like Georgetown Prep and Yale <i>and</i> Columbia, for that matter, play in fostering a culture in which “boys will be boys” and men like Kavanaugh are allegedly able to commit atrocious acts of violence against women under the protection of wealth and privilege.</p> <p>It’s hard not to see parallels on our own campus. After all, Columbia is a part of this <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/opinion/2018/10/16/discourse-debate-how-do-columbias-wealth-demographics-affect-student-life/">elite system</a>. It also has a very <a href="https://www.thecut.com/2014/09/emma-sulkowicz-campus-sexual-assault-activism.html?mid=twitter_nymag">public</a> <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/news/2014/12/04/history-red-tape/">history</a> of inadequately addressing sexual assault and harassment. A 2017 study found that <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/news/2017/11/10/one-in-three-columbia-undergraduate-women-face-sexual-assault-by-senior-year-study-finds/">one in three women</a> here experiences sexual assault by their senior year—a disproportionate figure compared to the <a href="https://www.womenshealth.gov/relationships-and-safety/sexual-assault-and-rape/college-sexual-assault">one in five statistic</a> reported by the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services’ Office on Women’s Health. Until a few years ago, students found responsible for violating gender-based misconduct policies <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/news/2015/11/08/good-standing-follow/">could serve as RAs</a> and <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/news/2017/02/14/columbia-changes-policy-to-ban-some-gender-based-misconduct-violators-from-being-tas/">As</a>. While sexual violence is not isolated to our campus, it is troubling to view Columbia’s mismanagement of the problem given <a href="https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/2020-election/biden-cracks-jokes-about-conduct-women-found-inappropriate-n991406">how</a> <a href="https://www.cnn.com/2018/09/19/politics/anita-hill-clarence-thomas-allegations-timeline/index.html">many</a> <a href="https://www.patheos.com/blogs/nosacredcows/2017/10/neil-degrasse-tyson-accused-of-rape/">powerful</a> <a href="https://www.glamour.com/story/five-women-accuse-james-franco-of-sexually-inappropriate-and-exploitative-behavior-in-new-report">men</a> <a href="https://www.wsj.com/articles/dozens-of-people-recount-pattern-of-sexual-misconduct-by-las-vegas-mogul-steve-wynn-1516985953">with</a> <a href="https://www.vox.com/a/sexual-harassment-assault-allegations-list/donald-trump">elite</a> <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/12/04/business/media/bill-oreilly-lawsuit-harassment.html">pedigrees</a> <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/eye-lead/2018/04/20/under-the-veil-the-silence-surrounding-sexual-harassment-in-labs-at-columbia/">allegedly</a> <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/news/2018/08/28/gs-dean-faces-50-million-lawsuit-for-alleged-manipulative-sexual-behavior/">don’t</a> <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/news/2017/12/19/professor-william-harris-retires-as-university-settles-sexual-harassment-lawsuit/">care</a> about consent.</p> <p>And I guess part of the reason I care so much is that I worry what I’ll do if my rapist is ever nominated to the Supreme Court. As inspiring as Ford’s willingness to testify was, Kavanaugh was still confirmed, which doesn’t give survivors a lot of faith in the system even if we had any to begin with.</p> <p>Before I go any further, I’d like all of you who know me for whom this comes as a shock to know that I don’t want you to take it personally that I didn’t tell you. Prior to writing this column, I could count on one hand the number of people who knew about the incident because I didn’t want anyone else to feel burdened by the weight of my trauma. But today, I share my story in recognition of <a href="https://www.nsvrc.org/saam">Sexual Assault Awareness Month</a> and to demonstrate that ending sexual violence requires tearing down environments that foster it rather than just teaching women self-defense.</p> <p>Columbia’s current system of “prevention” is designed to implicitly put the burden on would-be victims rather than on attackers. Think back to <a href="https://www.college.columbia.edu/alumni/news/campus-conversation-sexual-assault">“CU Step Up,”</a> the bystander-intervention training we all had to attend during NSOP. While well-intentioned, this training doesn’t go far enough in treating rape as <a href="https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/science-says-toxic-masculinity-more-than-alcohol-leads-to-sexual-assault/">a product of toxic masculinity</a>. Instead, it felt to me that the training focused on teaching women and bystanders to stop rape in the moment as opposed to actively working to destroy the environment that allows for rape to happen. While we must call out the creepy guy at the bar who just drugged his date’s drink, we should also work to call out toxic masculine behaviors—such as catcalling, <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/sports/2016/11/10/wrestling-team-under-investigation-after-racially-sexually-explicit-group-messages/">objectifying women</a>, and <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/opinion/2018/04/27/why-i-left-my-frat/">downplaying the severity</a> of sexual violence—that contribute to a culture in which this kind of violence is permitted.</p> <p>Perhaps this is partly why in the immediate wake of my attack, I felt a deep sense of shame. By not using the tools I’d been given to “prevent” an assault, I felt like I’d failed somehow. Adding in the fact that my attacker—like in <a href="https://www.nsvrc.org/statistics">80 percent</a> of rape cases—was someone I knew, I irrationally felt like I’d brought the assault on myself. Perhaps as some kind of defense mechanism, I tried to convince myself that it wasn’t rape. Taken together, it’s no wonder I, like <a href="https://www.rainn.org/statistics/criminal-justice-system">75 percent of survivors</a>, chose not to report it. Even though I knew what had occurred was rape, I didn’t think I’d be believed, and I certainly did not want to relive the trauma of it just for nothing to come out of it.</p> <p>But unresolved trauma doesn’t just go away, and healing doesn’t happen linearly. Some days I don’t think about my attack at all. Other days, the memory sits in the back of my mind, waiting to be brought to the fore by some trigger. Sometimes I have to make up excuses to leave a space, like an RA training session with Sexual Violence Response; or a Portuguese class spent talking about the intersections of race, poverty, and violence; or an art showcase about sex positivity. Sometimes the triggers don’t make sense, and sometimes things that sound triggering aren’t. The one constant is that “survivor” is now embedded in my identity. I can still describe my attack in detail years after the fact, and I’m sure that 30 years from now I’ll still remember, just like Ford does.</p> <p>Still, I hold onto hope that one day we won’t need pieces like this, and we won’t rely on brave people like Ford and Hill to relive their trauma for at least some of us to take sexual violence seriously. But to get to that point, we need to do more than intervene in the moment. We need to attack behaviors that promote rape culture and continue to push for improved resources for survivors. Check your internal biases. Correct your friends that joke about rape. Show them how their words contribute to the minimization of sexual violence, especially on a campus where it is all too common.</p> <p>Don’t let the future Brett Kavanaughs of the world off the hook.</p> <p><i>Laura Salgado is a senior at Columbia College studying political science. She stands with survivors and thinks you should too.
